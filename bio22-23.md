## Group A — Very Short Answer (1 mark each, any 10)

**(I) How has the classification system changed over time?**
Classification evolved from Aristotle's simple two-kingdom system (Plants/Animals) → Whittaker's five-kingdom system (Monera, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, Animalia) → Carl Woese's three-domain system (Archaea, Bacteria, Eukarya), based on increasing knowledge of cell structure, nutrition, and molecular (rRNA) data.

**(II) Transfer of hereditary characters from one generation to another is known as:**
**Heredity** (inheritance).

**(III) Vitamin deficiencies:**
i) Rickets — Vitamin D deficiency
ii) Scurvy — Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) deficiency

**(IV) Define enzymes with an example:**
Enzymes are biological catalysts (mostly proteins) that speed up biochemical reactions without being consumed, by lowering activation energy. Example: **Amylase** converts starch into maltose.

**(V) Enzyme that transcribes hnRNA in eukaryotes:**
**RNA Polymerase II**

**(VI) Number of essential amino acids in nature (for humans):**
**9** (Histidine, Isoleucine, Leucine, Lysine, Methionine, Phenylalanine, Threonine, Tryptophan, Valine)

**(VII) What are imperfect fungi?**
Fungi Imperfecti (Deuteromycetes) are fungi in which only the asexual (mitotic/conidial) stage is known; a sexual stage has not been observed or is absent. Example: *Penicillium*.

**(VIII) A sudden heritable change in the gene is known as:**
**Mutation**

**(IX) Why are carbohydrates generally optically active?**
Because most carbohydrates (monosaccharides) contain one or more **asymmetric (chiral) carbon atoms**, which can rotate the plane of polarized light either to the right (dextrorotatory) or left (levorotatory).

**(X) Enzymes secreted by the pancreas:**
Trypsin, Chymotrypsin, Pancreatic amylase, Pancreatic lipase, Carboxypeptidase.

**(XI) Why are diatoms called "pearls of the ocean"?**
Diatoms possess a cell wall (frustule) made of silica that is glass-like, ornamented, and glistens/shimmers in water, giving them a jewel-like appearance — hence the name. They are also major primary producers, contributing greatly to marine food chains and oxygen production, adding to their prized status.

**(XII) Which process is an exception to Mendel's Law?**
**D. Linkage**

### Q5. Explain Epistasis with a suitable example

Epistasis is a phenomenon in which one gene (at one locus) masks or suppresses the phenotypic expression of another gene at a **different, non-allelic locus**. This differs from dominance, which occurs between alleles of the *same* gene.

**Example — Recessive epistasis in Labrador coat color:**
Two genes control coat color:
- Gene **E/e**: controls whether pigment is deposited in the coat (E = pigment deposited, e = no pigment, coat is yellow regardless of other gene)
- Gene **B/b**: controls color of the pigment (B = black, b = brown/chocolate)

If a dog is homozygous recessive **ee**, it appears **yellow** no matter what alleles are present at the B locus — the "ee" genotype epistatically masks the B gene's expression. Only when at least one E allele is present does the B gene show its effect (black or brown). This gives a modified dihybrid ratio of **9:3:4** instead of the standard 9:3:3:1.

### Q6. Steps after enzyme-substrate complex formation until product formation

1. **Enzyme-Substrate (ES) complex formation:** The substrate binds to the enzyme's active site (lock-and-key or induced-fit model).
2. **Formation of transition state:** The ES complex undergoes conformational strain/distortion, lowering the activation energy needed and forming a high-energy transition state.
3. **Catalytic conversion:** Bonds in the substrate are broken/formed via catalytic mechanisms (acid-base catalysis, covalent catalysis, etc.), converting substrate into product while still bound to the enzyme (forming an Enzyme-Product, or EP, complex).
4. **Product release:** The enzyme releases the product(s), as its affinity for the product is much lower than for the substrate.
5. **Enzyme regeneration:** The enzyme returns to its original conformation, free to bind another substrate molecule and repeat the cycle.

Overall: E + S ⇌ ES → EP → E + P

### Q7. Characteristics of *E. coli*, *S. cerevisiae*, and *D. melanogaster* as model organisms

**Why model organisms matter:** They are easy to maintain, have short generation times, well-mapped genomes, and findings often generalize to other organisms including humans.

**1. *Escherichia coli* (bacterium):**
- Prokaryotic, single-celled, rod-shaped gram-negative bacterium
- Extremely short generation time (~20 minutes)
- Easy and inexpensive to culture in simple media
- Small, fully sequenced genome (~4.6 Mb, ~4,300 genes) with no introns
- Amenable to genetic manipulation (plasmids, transformation, recombinant DNA technology)
- Used to study: gene regulation (lac operon), DNA replication, protein expression (as an expression host), molecular cloning
- Limitation: prokaryotic, so cannot model eukaryotic processes like splicing, organelles, or development

**2. *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* (budding yeast):**
- Simplest eukaryotic model organism, unicellular fungus
- Short generation time (~90 minutes), easy to culture
- Can exist in both haploid and diploid states, useful for genetic studies (recessive/dominant mutation analysis)
- Fully sequenced genome (~12 Mb, ~6,000 genes), first eukaryotic genome sequenced
- Has membrane-bound organelles, true nucleus, and cell-cycle machinery homologous to higher eukaryotes
- Used to study: cell cycle regulation, cell division, aging, protein trafficking, and eukaryotic gene expression
- Genetically tractable: easy homologous recombination allows precise gene knockouts

**3. *Drosophila melanogaster* (fruit fly):**
- Multicellular, invertebrate eukaryote model
- Short generation time (~10–14 days), produces large numbers of offspring
- Small, well-characterized genome (~180 Mb, ~4 chromosome pairs) with giant polytene chromosomes useful for cytological study
- Easy, cheap to maintain in the lab
- Used to study: classical genetics (Morgan's work on linkage/sex-linked inheritance), developmental biology (homeotic/Hox genes), neurobiology, behavior
- Has well-defined mutant phenotypes (e.g., white eyes, vestigial wings) useful for teaching and research in Mendelian and chromosomal genetics

### Q8. Hierarchy of DNA structure — from single strand to double helix to nucleosomes

1. **Primary structure — Polynucleotide chain (single strand):**
   Nucleotides (deoxyribose sugar + phosphate + nitrogenous base) are linked via 3'-5' phosphodiester bonds forming a single-stranded polynucleotide chain with directionality (5' to 3').

2. **Secondary structure — Double helix:**
   Two antiparallel polynucleotide strands pair via complementary hydrogen bonding (A-T: 2 H-bonds; G-C: 3 H-bonds), following Chargaff's rules. The strands twist into the right-handed **Watson-Crick B-DNA double helix**, with a diameter of ~2 nm, ~10 base pairs per turn, and major/minor grooves.

3. **Tertiary structure — Supercoiling:**
   The double helix can be further twisted upon itself (supercoiling), which helps compact DNA and is regulated by topoisomerases; important in circular bacterial DNA and in eukaryotic chromatin packaging.

4. **Nucleosome formation (chromatin level):**
   In eukaryotes, ~146–147 bp of DNA wraps almost twice around a **histone octamer** (2 copies each of H2A, H2B, H3, H4), forming the fundamental unit of chromatin — the **nucleosome** ("beads on a string" structure). Linker DNA (with histone H1) connects adjacent nucleosomes.

5. **Higher-order packaging:**
   Nucleosome chains coil into a **30 nm chromatin fiber** (solenoid model), which loops further into **300 nm chromatin loops**, then condenses into a **700 nm chromatid**, ultimately forming a fully condensed **metaphase chromosome** (~1400 nm), achieving nearly 10,000-fold compaction of the original DNA length.

This hierarchical packaging allows ~2 meters of DNA to fit inside a nucleus only a few micrometers wide, while still allowing regulated access for replication and transcription.

### Q9. How would you convey that Biology is as important a scientific discipline as Mathematics, Physics, and Chemistry?

**1. Biology underpins human survival and health:**
Advances in medicine, vaccines, antibiotics, genetics, and biotechnology directly stem from biological research — arguably having the most direct impact on human life expectancy and quality of life of any science.

**2. Biology integrates and applies the other sciences:**
Modern biology is deeply quantitative and interdisciplinary — it uses **physics** (biophysics, imaging, biomechanics), **chemistry** (biochemistry, molecular structure, enzyme kinetics), and **mathematics** (population modeling, bioinformatics, systems biology, statistics in genetics). This shows biology is not "softer" but a synthesis discipline requiring rigor from all three.

**3. Biology has its own universal laws and predictive theories:**
Just as physics has laws of motion and chemistry the periodic law, biology has unifying principles like the **theory of evolution by natural selection**, the **central dogma of molecular biology** (DNA→RNA→protein), **Mendelian laws of inheritance**, and **cell theory** — each with strong predictive and explanatory power.

**4. Biology drives modern technology and economy:**
Fields like genetic engineering, CRISPR gene editing, synthetic biology, agriculture (Green Revolution), and biotechnology-based industries (pharmaceuticals, biofuels) show biology's direct economic and technological relevance, comparable to engineering applications of physics/chemistry.

**5. Complexity and precision:**
Biological systems, from a single cell to entire ecosystems, involve immense complexity, requiring sophisticated experimental design, mathematical modeling (e.g., epidemiological models, ecological modeling), and rigorous quantitative methods — dispelling the notion that biology is merely descriptive or "soft science."

**6. Nobel recognition and research investment:**
Biology and its related fields (Physiology/Medicine) receive dedicated Nobel Prizes and massive global research funding, reflecting the scientific community's view of its foundational importance alongside physics and chemistry.

**Conclusion:** Biology is not a lesser or purely descriptive science — it is a rigorous, quantitative, and interdisciplinary field with its own fundamental laws, immense societal impact, and deep reliance on mathematics, physics, and chemistry, making it an equally essential pillar of science.

### Q10. Characteristics of *C. elegans*, *A. thaliana*, and *M. musculus* as model organisms

**1. *Caenorhabditis elegans* (nematode worm):**
- Small (~1 mm), transparent, multicellular eukaryote — allows direct microscopic observation of every cell during development
- Short life cycle (~3 days) and produces many offspring (self-fertilizing hermaphrodite)
- Simple, fixed number of somatic cells (959 in adult hermaphrodite) with completely mapped **cell lineage** (every cell division from zygote to adult is known)
- Fully sequenced genome (first multicellular organism genome sequenced, ~100 Mb)
- Has a simple nervous system (302 neurons) with fully mapped connectome
- Used to study: developmental biology, apoptosis (programmed cell death), aging, neurobiology, RNA interference (RNAi discovered in *C. elegans*)

**2. *Arabidopsis thaliana* (thale cress):**
- Small flowering plant (model for plant biology), member of the mustard family
- Short generation time (~6 weeks), produces thousands of seeds per plant
- Small genome for a plant (~135 Mb), first plant genome to be fully sequenced
- Easy to grow in small spaces in the lab, self-pollinating
- Amenable to genetic transformation (via *Agrobacterium tumefaciens*)
- Used to study: plant development, flowering time control, photosynthesis-related genetics, plant hormone signaling, and plant response to environmental stress

**3. *Mus musculus* (house mouse):**
- Mammalian model organism, physiologically and genetically closer to humans than the above
- Relatively short generation time (~3 weeks gestation, sexual maturity ~6–8 weeks) compared to other mammals
- Fully sequenced genome, highly similar to the human genome (~99% of human genes have a mouse counterpart)
- Easily manipulated genetically — transgenic and knockout mice are widely used
- Used to study: mammalian development, immunology, cancer biology, neuroscience, and as a preclinical model for testing drugs and disease mechanisms before human trials

### Q11. How competitive, uncompetitive, and non-competitive inhibitors act on Km and Vmax

Enzyme inhibition is analyzed using the **Michaelis-Menten equation** and **Lineweaver-Burk plots**. Km = substrate concentration at half-maximal velocity (inverse measure of enzyme-substrate affinity); Vmax = maximum reaction velocity at saturating substrate.

**1. Competitive Inhibition:**
- The inhibitor structurally resembles the substrate and binds **reversibly to the active site**, competing directly with the substrate.
- Since the inhibitor and substrate compete for the same site, increasing substrate concentration can outcompete the inhibitor and still achieve Vmax.
- **Effect:** Km **increases** (apparent lower substrate affinity), **Vmax remains unchanged**.
- Example: Malonate competitively inhibiting succinate dehydrogenase.

**2. Uncompetitive Inhibition:**
- The inhibitor binds **only to the enzyme-substrate (ES) complex**, not to the free enzyme, at a site other than the active site.
- Because it only binds after substrate has bound, increasing substrate concentration does not reverse inhibition — in fact, more ES complex means more inhibitor binding.
- **Effect:** Both **Km and Vmax decrease** proportionally (Km decreases because the inhibitor pulls the equilibrium toward ES formation via Le Chatelier's principle; Vmax decreases because some ES-I complex cannot form product).

**3. Non-competitive Inhibition:**
- The inhibitor binds to an **allosteric site** on the enzyme, with equal affinity for both the free enzyme (E) and the enzyme-substrate complex (ES), independent of substrate binding.
- It doesn't block substrate binding but changes the enzyme's conformation, reducing catalytic efficiency.
- Since substrate can still bind normally, **Km remains unchanged**, but because some fraction of enzyme is always inhibited regardless of substrate concentration, **Vmax decreases**.

**Summary Table:**

| Inhibitor type | Binds to | Effect on Km | Effect on Vmax |
|---|---|---|---|
| Competitive | Free enzyme (active site) | Increases | Unchanged |
| Uncompetitive | ES complex only | Decreases | Decreases |
| Non-competitive | Both E and ES (allosteric site) | Unchanged | Decreases |
