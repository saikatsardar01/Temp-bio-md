**(VII) What is a cistron?**
A cistron is the segment of DNA (functional unit of a gene) that codes for a single polypeptide chain; it is the smallest genetic unit whose alteration produces a mutant phenotype (defined by the cis-trans/complementation test).

(Other valid answers: antibiotic production, biogas production, industrial fermentation.)

**(IX) Main function of kinase:**
Kinases catalyze the **transfer of a phosphate group** (commonly from ATP) to a substrate molecule (protein, lipid, or other), a process called **phosphorylation**, which regulates enzyme activity, signal transduction, and metabolic pathways.

**(XI) Are viruses living or non-living?**
Viruses are considered to be at the **borderline of living and non-living**. They show no independent metabolism and cannot reproduce outside a host cell (non-living characteristic), but once inside a host cell, they replicate, mutate, and evolve using the host's machinery (living characteristic).

### Q2. How are co-factors different from prosthetic groups?

Both cofactors and prosthetic groups are non-protein components required for enzyme activity, but they differ in the strength/permanence of their association with the enzyme.

| Feature | Co-factor (general term) | Prosthetic group |
|---|---|---|
| Definition | Any non-protein component (ion or organic molecule) needed for enzyme activity | A type of cofactor that is **tightly/covalently bound** to the enzyme |
| Binding | Can be loosely bound (dissociable) | Permanently/tightly bound |
| Types | Includes metal ions (Mg²⁺, Zn²⁺) and coenzymes (loosely bound organic molecules like NAD⁺) | Always tightly bound organic/inorganic component |
| Removability | Can dissociate from the enzyme after the reaction | Remains attached to the enzyme even when not catalyzing |
| Example | Mg²⁺ in kinases (loosely associated) | **FAD** in succinate dehydrogenase; **heme** group in cytochromes/catalase |

In short: **prosthetic groups are a subset of cofactors** — the difference lies in the strength and permanence of binding to the enzyme.

### Q3. Characteristics of genetic code

1. **Triplet code:** Each codon consists of three nucleotides (a triplet) that specify one amino acid.
2. **Degenerate (redundant):** Most amino acids are coded for by more than one codon (e.g., Leucine has 6 codons), providing a buffer against mutations.
3. **Non-overlapping:** Each nucleotide is read only once, as part of only one codon; the reading frame does not overlap.
4. **Commaless (continuous):** There are no punctuation marks between codons; the code is read continuously from a fixed starting point.
5. **Unambiguous:** A specific codon always codes for the same amino acid (no codon codes for more than one amino acid).
6. **Universal:** The genetic code is (with rare exceptions) the same across nearly all organisms, from bacteria to humans, reflecting a common evolutionary origin.
7. **Start and stop codons:** AUG serves as the start codon (also codes for Methionine); UAA, UAG, UGA are stop (nonsense) codons that signal termination of translation.
8. **Colinearity:** The linear sequence of codons in mRNA corresponds directly to the linear sequence of amino acids in the resulting polypeptide.

### Q4. Differentiate between DNA and RNA

| Feature | DNA | RNA |
|---|---|---|
| Sugar | Deoxyribose (lacks OH at 2' carbon) | Ribose (has OH at 2' carbon) |
| Strands | Double-stranded (usually) | Single-stranded (usually) |
| Nitrogenous bases | Adenine, Guanine, Cytosine, **Thymine** | Adenine, Guanine, Cytosine, **Uracil** |
| Stability | More stable (due to deoxyribose and double helix) | Less stable, more prone to degradation |
| Function | Stores and transmits genetic information (long-term storage) | Involved in protein synthesis (mRNA, tRNA, rRNA); acts as messenger/functional molecule |
| Location | Mainly in nucleus (also mitochondria/chloroplast) | Nucleus and cytoplasm |
| Replication | Self-replicating | Synthesized from DNA template (transcription); does not typically self-replicate |

### Q5. Explain Krebs cycle with a suitable flowchart

The **Krebs cycle (Citric Acid Cycle/TCA cycle)** occurs in the mitochondrial matrix and is the central pathway of aerobic respiration, oxidizing acetyl-CoA to CO₂ while generating energy carriers (NADH, FADH₂) for the electron transport chain.

**Key steps:**
1. Acetyl-CoA (2C) combines with Oxaloacetate (4C) to form **Citrate (6C)**, catalyzed by citrate synthase.
2. Citrate is isomerized to **Isocitrate**.
3. Isocitrate is oxidatively decarboxylated to **α-Ketoglutarate (5C)**, releasing CO₂ and producing NADH.
4. α-Ketoglutarate is oxidatively decarboxylated to **Succinyl-CoA (4C)**, releasing another CO₂ and producing NADH.
5. Succinyl-CoA is converted to **Succinate**, generating one **GTP/ATP** (substrate-level phosphorylation).
6. Succinate is oxidized to **Fumarate**, producing **FADH₂**.
7. Fumarate is hydrated to **Malate**.
8. Malate is oxidized back to **Oxaloacetate**, producing another NADH, regenerating the starting molecule to continue the cycle.

**Net yield per acetyl-CoA:** 3 NADH + 1 FADH₂ + 1 GTP(ATP) + 2 CO₂

**Flowchart:**
```
Acetyl-CoA (2C) + Oxaloacetate (4C)
            │
            ▼
        Citrate (6C)
            │
            ▼
        Isocitrate (6C)
            │  → releases CO₂, forms NADH
            ▼
      α-Ketoglutarate (5C)
            │  → releases CO₂, forms NADH
            ▼
       Succinyl-CoA (4C)
            │  → forms GTP/ATP
            ▼
        Succinate (4C)
            │  → forms FADH₂
            ▼
        Fumarate (4C)
            │
            ▼
         Malate (4C)
            │  → forms NADH
            ▼
      Oxaloacetate (4C)  ──► (cycle repeats)
```

### Q6. Coding strand given as 3' AATGCAGCTATTAGG 5' — find complementary strand and mRNA

**Given coding strand (3'→5'):**
`3' - AAT GCA GCT ATT AGG - 5'`

**1. Complementary strand (template strand):**
The complementary strand pairs base-for-base (A↔T, G↔C) and runs antiparallel (5'→3'):

`5' - TTA CGT CGA TAA TCC - 3'`

**2. mRNA sequence:**
The mRNA is synthesized using the **template strand** as the guide, and is identical to the coding strand except **T is replaced by U**:

`5' - AAU GCA GCU AUU AGG - 3'`

*(Note: the mRNA has the same sequence and same 5'→3' polarity as the given coding strand, with U substituted for T.)*

### Q7. Illustrate the two models by which an enzyme holds the substrate

Two classical models explain how an enzyme's active site binds its specific substrate:

**1. Lock and Key model (Emil Fischer, 1894):**
- The enzyme's active site has a rigid, pre-formed shape that is exactly complementary to the shape of the specific substrate, just as a specific key fits only its matching lock.
- The enzyme and substrate fit together precisely without any change in shape upon binding.
- **Limitation:** This model does not explain the flexibility of enzymes or how they can sometimes act on more than one related substrate, nor does it account for the dynamic nature of proteins.

```
   Enzyme active site        Substrate
      ⌐⌐⌐___⌐⌐⌐              ▭▭▭
      |         |     +      ▭▭▭     →   Perfect Fit (like key in lock)
      ⌐⌐⌐───⌐⌐⌐
```

**2. Induced Fit model (Daniel Koshland, 1958):**
- The active site of the enzyme is **flexible**, not rigid. It does not perfectly match the substrate shape initially.
- Upon substrate binding, the enzyme undergoes a **conformational change**, molding/adjusting its active site around the substrate to achieve a snug fit — much like a "glove" adjusting to fit a "hand."
- This induced conformational change also helps bring catalytic residues into the correct alignment for the reaction, aiding catalysis.
- This model better explains enzyme flexibility, specificity, and catalytic efficiency, and is more widely accepted today.

```
   Enzyme (before binding)         Substrate
        ___                          
       /   \      +      ▭▭▭   →   Enzyme changes shape to fit substrate (induced fit)
      |     |
       \___/
```

**Summary:** The lock-and-key model illustrates rigid, precise complementarity, while the induced fit model illustrates a dynamic, flexible active site that reshapes itself upon substrate binding — the latter being the more accurate representation of enzyme-substrate interaction in modern biochemistry.

### Q8. Explain the steps of Glycolysis in detail

**Glycolysis** (Embden-Meyerhof-Parnas pathway) is the metabolic pathway that breaks down one molecule of glucose (6C) into two molecules of pyruvate (3C), occurring in the cytoplasm and not requiring oxygen. It consists of two phases: the **Energy Investment Phase** and the **Energy Payoff Phase**.

**Energy Investment Phase (uses 2 ATP):**
1. **Glucose → Glucose-6-phosphate:** Catalyzed by **hexokinase**, using 1 ATP (phosphorylation traps glucose inside the cell).
2. **Glucose-6-phosphate → Fructose-6-phosphate:** Isomerization by phosphoglucose isomerase.
3. **Fructose-6-phosphate → Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate:** Catalyzed by **phosphofructokinase (PFK)**, using another 1 ATP (key regulatory/rate-limiting step).
4. **Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate → splits into two 3-carbon molecules:** Dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP) and Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P), catalyzed by aldolase.
5. **DHAP is converted to G3P** by triose phosphate isomerase, so both molecules proceed identically through the next steps — **from here, all subsequent steps occur twice per original glucose molecule.**

**Energy Payoff Phase (produces 4 ATP + 2 NADH, per glucose):**
6. **G3P → 1,3-Bisphosphoglycerate:** Oxidation and phosphorylation by glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase, producing **NADH** (reduces NAD⁺).
7. **1,3-Bisphosphoglycerate → 3-Phosphoglycerate:** Substrate-level phosphorylation produces **1 ATP** (via phosphoglycerate kinase).
8. **3-Phosphoglycerate → 2-Phosphoglycerate:** Isomerization by phosphoglycerate mutase.
9. **2-Phosphoglycerate → Phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP):** Dehydration by enolase, forming a high-energy phosphate bond.
10. **PEP → Pyruvate:** Substrate-level phosphorylation by pyruvate kinase, producing **1 ATP**.

**Net yield (per glucose molecule):**
- Glucose (6C) → 2 Pyruvate (3C each)
- **Net ATP: 2** (4 produced − 2 invested)
- **Net NADH: 2**

**Fate of pyruvate:** Under aerobic conditions, pyruvate enters the mitochondria and is converted to Acetyl-CoA for the Krebs cycle. Under anaerobic conditions, it undergoes fermentation (to lactate in animals, or ethanol + CO₂ in yeast).

### Q9. Functions of proteins as receptors and structural elements

**1. Proteins as receptors:**
Receptor proteins are embedded in the cell membrane (or located intracellularly) and bind specific signaling molecules (ligands — hormones, neurotransmitters, growth factors), triggering a cellular response.
- **Mechanism:** Ligand binding causes a conformational change in the receptor, initiating a signal transduction cascade (often via second messengers like cAMP, or phosphorylation cascades) that alters cell behavior, gene expression, or metabolism.
- **Examples:**
  - **G-protein coupled receptors (GPCRs):** Bind hormones/neurotransmitters (e.g., adrenaline receptor), activating intracellular G-proteins.
  - **Insulin receptor:** A receptor tyrosine kinase that binds insulin, regulating glucose uptake into cells.
  - **Neurotransmitter receptors:** e.g., acetylcholine receptors at neuromuscular junctions, enabling nerve-to-muscle signal transmission.
- **Significance:** Receptors allow cells to sense and respond to their external environment, coordinate multicellular communication, and regulate processes like immune response, hormone signaling, and sensory perception.

**2. Proteins as structural elements:**
Structural proteins provide mechanical support, shape, and strength to cells, tissues, and organisms.
- **Collagen:** The most abundant protein in the human body; provides tensile strength to skin, tendons, ligaments, bones, and connective tissue.
- **Keratin:** Forms hair, nails, feathers, and the outer layer of skin, providing protection and rigidity.
- **Elastin:** Provides elasticity to tissues like blood vessels, lungs, and skin, allowing them to stretch and recoil.
- **Actin and Myosin:** Structural/contractile proteins forming the cytoskeleton and muscle fibers, enabling cell shape maintenance, intracellular transport, and muscle contraction.
- **Tubulin:** Forms microtubules, part of the cytoskeleton, involved in maintaining cell shape, intracellular transport, and forming the mitotic spindle during cell division.

**Significance:** Structural proteins give cells and tissues their shape, mechanical integrity, and ability to withstand physical stress, forming the "scaffolding" of biological systems — complementing the dynamic signaling role of receptor proteins.

### Q10. Characteristics of individuals with chromosomal abnormalities: Trisomy 21, XXY, XO

**1. Trisomy at chromosome 21 (Down Syndrome):**
- Caused by the presence of an **extra copy of chromosome 21** (three copies instead of two), usually due to non-disjunction during meiosis.
- **Characteristics:**
  - Intellectual disability (varying degree)
  - Distinctive facial features: flat facial profile, upward slanting eyes (epicanthic folds), small ears
  - Short stature, short neck, and a single palmar crease (simian crease)
  - Hypotonia (reduced muscle tone) at birth
  - Increased risk of congenital heart defects, hearing/vision problems
  - Increased risk of early-onset Alzheimer's disease and leukemia
  - Higher incidence with increasing maternal age

**2. XXY (Klinefelter Syndrome):**
- Caused by the presence of an **extra X chromosome in males** (47, XXY instead of 46, XY), due to non-disjunction of sex chromosomes.
- **Characteristics:**
  - Phenotypically male but with underdeveloped testes (hypogonadism), leading to reduced testosterone
  - Infertility (most common cause of male infertility due to chromosomal abnormality)
  - Taller than average stature with longer limbs
  - Reduced facial/body hair, gynecomastia (breast tissue development)
  - Learning difficulties or mild developmental delays in some cases
  - Generally normal life expectancy, though some health risks (osteoporosis, metabolic syndrome) may be elevated

**3. XO (Turner Syndrome):**
- Caused by the **complete or partial absence of one X chromosome in females** (45, X instead of 46, XX).
- **Characteristics:**
  - Short stature
  - Webbed neck, low-set ears, and a broad/shield-like chest
  - Underdeveloped ovaries (gonadal dysgenesis), leading to infertility and lack of secondary sexual characteristics without hormone therapy
  - Congenital heart defects (e.g., coarctation of the aorta) and kidney abnormalities are common
  - Normal intelligence in most cases, though some may have specific learning difficulties (e.g., spatial reasoning)
  - Requires hormone replacement therapy for pubertal development

### Q11. Dihybrid cross: Tall red-flowered (dominant) × Dwarf white-flowered (recessive); dihybrid ratio; effect of gene interaction

**Setup:**
Let: **T** = Tall (dominant), **t** = dwarf (recessive); **R** = Red flower (dominant), **r** = white flower (recessive).

Assume the tall, red-flowered parent is homozygous dominant (**TTRR**) and the dwarf, white-flowered parent is homozygous recessive (**ttrr**) — the classic dihybrid cross setup.

**P generation:** TTRR (Tall, Red) × ttrr (dwarf, white)

**F1 generation:** All offspring are **TtRr** (Tall, Red) — showing that Tall and Red are dominant traits (uniformity of F1, per Mendel's Law of Dominance).

**F1 × F1 (self-cross) → F2 generation:**
Each F1 parent (TtRr) produces four types of gametes: **TR, Tr, tR, tr** (Law of Independent Assortment).

**Punnett Square (F2):**

|  | TR | Tr | tR | tr |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **TR** | TTRR | TTRr | TtRR | TtRr |
| **Tr** | TTRr | TTrr | TtRr | Ttrr |
| **tR** | TtRR | TtRr | ttRR | ttRr |
| **tr** | TtRr | Ttrr | ttRr | ttrr |

**Phenotypic classes and ratio (standard, independent assortment):**
- Tall, Red: 9
- Tall, white: 3
- dwarf, Red: 3
- dwarf, white: 1

**Dihybrid ratio = 9 : 3 : 3 : 1**

**Effect if the two genes interact with each other (gene interaction/epistasis):**

If the genes for plant height and flower color are **not independent** but interact (epistasis), the standard 9:3:3:1 ratio gets modified into other characteristic ratios depending on the type of interaction, such as:
- **9:3:4** (recessive epistasis) — if, for example, being homozygous recessive at one locus (tt) masks the flower color phenotype, making both "dwarf, red" and "dwarf, white" categories appear as a single "dwarf" phenotype regardless of flower color.
- **12:3:1** (dominant epistasis) — if a dominant allele at one locus masks the expression of the other gene.
- **9:7** (complementary gene action) — if both dominant alleles (T_R_) are needed together to produce one phenotype (e.g., red flowers appear only when both dominant alleles are present, and all other combinations give white/dwarf phenotype).
- **15:1** (duplicate genes with cumulative effect) — if either dominant allele alone can produce the same phenotype.

**Conclusion:** Without gene interaction, independent assortment of the two genes for height and flower color produces the classic **9:3:3:1** ratio. However, if the genes interact (epistatically), the phenotypic classes merge or shift, producing modified ratios (e.g., 9:3:4, 12:3:1, 9:7, or 15:1) instead of four fully distinct phenotypic classes — since one gene's expression now depends on or is masked by the genotype at the other locus.
