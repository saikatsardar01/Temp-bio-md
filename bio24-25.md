### Q2. Types of culture media based on physical state; agar vs. broth

Culture media can be classified based on physical consistency into three types:

1. **Liquid media (Broth):** No solidifying agent added; nutrients are dissolved in liquid (e.g., water-based solution). Used for rapid, uniform growth of large numbers of microorganisms, e.g., Nutrient broth, Peptone water.
2. **Solid media:** Contains a solidifying agent (usually **agar**, ~1.5–2%) which gels the medium, allowing colonies to grow as discrete, visible spots on the surface. Used for isolation, colony morphology study, and pure culture techniques (e.g., streak plate method).
3. **Semi-solid media:** Contains a lower concentration of agar (~0.2–0.5%), giving a soft, gel-like consistency. Used to study bacterial motility or for maintaining stock cultures (e.g., motility test medium).

**Agar vs. Broth:**

| Feature | Agar (Solid Medium) | Broth (Liquid Medium) |
|---|---|---|
| Physical state | Solid/gel | Liquid |
| Solidifying agent | Agar-agar (polysaccharide from red algae) | None |
| Growth pattern | Discrete, visible colonies | Uniform turbidity/cloudiness |
| Use | Isolation, colony characteristics, pure culture | Mass cultivation, biochemical tests, growth curve studies |
| Example | Nutrient agar, MacConkey agar | Nutrient broth, Luria broth |

### Q3. Comparison between eye and camera

The human eye and a camera work on similar optical principles — both focus light onto a light-sensitive surface to form an image.

| Feature | Eye | Camera |
|---|---|---|
| Light-focusing structure | Cornea + Lens | Convex lens system |
| Aperture control | Iris (adjusts pupil size) | Diaphragm/aperture setting |
| Light-sensitive surface | Retina (rod and cone cells) | Photographic film / digital sensor (CCD/CMOS) |
| Focusing mechanism | Lens changes shape/curvature (accommodation via ciliary muscles) | Lens moves back and forth (change in distance) |
| Image formed | Real, inverted (corrected by the brain) | Real, inverted |
| Light-sensitive chemical | Rhodopsin (visual purple) in rod cells | Light-sensitive photographic emulsion/silicon sensor |
| Continuous operation | Continuously active, self-cleaning (tears), self-focusing | Operates only when shutter is triggered |
| Image processing | Processed and interpreted by the brain (optic nerve) | Processed by internal circuitry/software |

### Q5. Short note on Central Dogma

The **Central Dogma of Molecular Biology**, proposed by Francis Crick (1958), describes the unidirectional flow of genetic information within a biological system:

**DNA → RNA → Protein**

1. **Replication:** DNA makes a copy of itself (DNA → DNA), ensuring genetic continuity across generations.
2. **Transcription:** Genetic information in DNA is copied into messenger RNA (mRNA) by RNA polymerase (DNA → RNA), occurring in the nucleus in eukaryotes.
3. **Translation:** The mRNA sequence is decoded by ribosomes (with the help of tRNA) to synthesize a specific polypeptide chain/protein (RNA → Protein), occurring in the cytoplasm.

Later discoveries (e.g., **reverse transcription** in retroviruses, where RNA is converted back into DNA) added an exception to the strict unidirectional flow, but the core dogma remains foundational to understanding gene expression: genetic information generally flows from DNA to RNA to protein, and not in reverse (proteins cannot code back into nucleic acids).

### Q6. Types of lipids depending on esterification

Lipids can be classified based on whether/how they undergo esterification (bond formation between fatty acid and alcohol groups):

1. **Simple lipids:** Esters of fatty acids with various alcohols.
   - **Fats and oils (Triglycerides/Triacylglycerols):** Esters of glycerol with three fatty acid molecules. Fats are solid at room temperature (saturated), oils are liquid (unsaturated).
   - **Waxes:** Esters of fatty acids with long-chain monohydric alcohols (not glycerol).

2. **Compound (Complex) lipids:** Esters of fatty acids containing additional groups besides alcohol and fatty acid.
   - **Phospholipids:** Contain a phosphate group (e.g., lecithin) — major component of cell membranes.
   - **Glycolipids:** Contain a carbohydrate group attached (no phosphate).
   - **Lipoproteins:** Lipids combined with proteins, used in lipid transport in blood (e.g., LDL, HDL).

3. **Derived lipids:** Substances derived from hydrolysis of simple or compound lipids, which may or may not retain general lipid characteristics.
   - Includes fatty acids, glycerol, steroids (e.g., cholesterol), and terpenes.
   - These are not directly formed by esterification themselves but arise from breakdown of esterified lipids.

### Q7. (a) Phases of microbial growth kinetics with graph [10] (b) Sterilization vs. Pasteurization [5]

**(a) Phases of microbial growth (bacterial growth curve):**

When bacteria are inoculated into a fresh liquid medium (batch culture), the population size over time (typically plotted as log of viable cell number vs. time) shows four distinct phases:

1. **Lag phase:** Bacteria adapt to the new environment — synthesizing enzymes, RNA, and other components needed for division. Little to no increase in cell number; cells increase in size/metabolic activity.
2. **Log (Exponential) phase:** Cells divide at a constant, maximum rate (doubling at regular intervals). Population increases exponentially; this phase is used to study generation time and is most sensitive to antibiotics/environmental stress.
3. **Stationary phase:** Growth rate slows and plateaus as nutrients become depleted and waste products/toxins accumulate. Rate of new cell formation equals rate of cell death — net population size remains constant.
4. **Death (Decline) phase:** Nutrients are exhausted and toxic metabolites accumulate to lethal levels; cells die faster than new ones form, causing exponential decline in viable population.

**Graph description:** X-axis = Time; Y-axis = Log (number of viable cells). The curve rises slowly (lag), rises steeply/linearly on log scale (log phase), plateaus (stationary), then declines (death phase) — giving a characteristic S-shaped then downward-curving sigmoid pattern.

```
Log (viable cell count)
   |                    ______________
   |                   /              \
   |                  /                \
   |                 /                  \
   |                /                    \___
   |    __________ /
   |___/  
   +--------------------------------------------→ Time
     Lag      Log(Exponential)   Stationary   Death
```

**(b) Sterilization vs. Pasteurization:**

| Feature | Sterilization | Pasteurization |
|---|---|---|
| Definition | Complete destruction/removal of ALL forms of microbial life, including spores | Mild heat treatment killing pathogenic and most spoilage vegetative microbes (not spores) |
| Temperature used | High (e.g., autoclaving at 121°C, 15 psi) | Moderate (e.g., 63°C for 30 min or 72°C for 15 sec — HTST) |
| Effect on spores | Destroys spores | Spores survive |
| Purpose | Used for surgical instruments, culture media, lab equipment | Used for food preservation (milk, juices) while retaining nutritional/sensory quality |
| Nutrient/quality impact | May degrade heat-sensitive materials | Preserves taste, nutrients better since less harsh |

### Q8. Why do engineers need to study biology?

1. **Biomimicry and Bio-inspired design:** Many engineering innovations are inspired by biological systems — e.g., aircraft wing design inspired by bird flight, Velcro inspired by burr plants, robotics inspired by insect locomotion. Understanding biology helps engineers innovate more efficient, adaptive designs.

2. **Biomedical Engineering:** Fields like prosthetics, medical imaging (MRI, CT), artificial organs, implants, and diagnostic devices require engineers to understand human physiology and anatomy to design compatible, functional, and safe devices.

3. **Bioinformatics and Computational Biology:** With the explosion of biological data (genomics, proteomics), computer/software engineers are needed to design algorithms, databases, and AI systems to analyze DNA sequences, protein structures, and biological networks.

4. **Environmental and Genetic Engineering:** Chemical and environmental engineers apply biological principles in wastewater treatment (using microorganisms), biofuel production, and genetic engineering (recombinant DNA technology, CRISPR) for agriculture and pharmaceuticals.

5. **Biotechnology industries:** Industrial/chemical engineers design and scale up bioreactors for fermentation processes (antibiotics, insulin, vaccines production), requiring knowledge of microbial growth kinetics and enzyme biochemistry.

6. **Sustainability and biomaterials:** Engineers increasingly rely on biological materials (biodegradable polymers, biomaterials for tissue engineering) to design eco-friendly and sustainable products, requiring an understanding of biological compatibility and degradation.

7. **Systems thinking:** Biological systems are excellent models for complex, self-regulating, feedback-controlled systems, offering engineers insight into robust system design principles (e.g., homeostasis models applied to control systems engineering).

8. **Ethical and safety considerations:** As technology increasingly intersects with living systems (biotech, genetic engineering, AI in healthcare), engineers must understand biological consequences to design ethically and safely.

**Conclusion:** As technology and biology increasingly converge (bioengineering, synthetic biology, health-tech), a foundational understanding of biology equips engineers to innovate responsibly and effectively across nearly every modern engineering discipline.

### Q9. (a) What is DNA replication? [2] (b) Mechanism of DNA replication with diagram [13]

**(a) DNA Replication:**
DNA replication is the biological process by which a DNA molecule produces two identical copies of itself. It is **semi-conservative** — each daughter DNA molecule consists of one original (parental) strand and one newly synthesized strand, ensuring accurate transmission of genetic information to daughter cells.

**(b) Mechanism of DNA replication:**

1. **Initiation:**
   - Replication begins at specific sites called **origins of replication** (oriC in bacteria).
   - **Helicase** unwinds the double helix by breaking hydrogen bonds between base pairs, creating a **replication fork**.
   - **Single-strand binding proteins (SSBs)** stabilize the separated single strands, preventing them from re-annealing.
   - **Topoisomerase (DNA gyrase)** relieves the torsional strain/supercoiling ahead of the fork caused by unwinding.

2. **Primer synthesis:**
   - DNA polymerase cannot initiate synthesis de novo; **primase** synthesizes a short RNA primer to provide a free 3'-OH group for DNA polymerase to extend.

3. **Elongation:**
   - **DNA Polymerase III** (in prokaryotes) adds nucleotides in the 5'→3' direction, complementary to the template strand.
   - **Leading strand:** Synthesized continuously in the same direction as the replication fork movement.
   - **Lagging strand:** Synthesized discontinuously, in short **Okazaki fragments**, in the direction opposite to fork movement (each fragment requires its own RNA primer).

4. **Primer removal and gap filling:**
   - **DNA Polymerase I** removes RNA primers and fills the resulting gaps with DNA nucleotides.

5. **Ligation:**
   - **DNA ligase** seals the remaining nicks between Okazaki fragments (and the final gap on the leading strand), forming a continuous DNA strand by forming phosphodiester bonds.

**Diagram description:** Show a replication fork with helicase unwinding the parental double helix at the fork point, SSB proteins coating single strands, primase laying RNA primers, DNA Pol III extending leading strand continuously toward the fork and lagging strand as discontinuous Okazaki fragments (with primers) moving away from the fork, and DNA ligase joining fragments.

```
              Leading strand (continuous synthesis) →
        5'───────────────────────────────────→ 3'
   3' ◄──────────────╲                              
                       ╲  Helicase (unwinds)         
   5' ────────────────── ╲                          
        Okazaki fragments ← ← ←  (Lagging strand, discontinuous)
```

### Q10. (a) Secondary structure of protein [5] (b) Essential, conditionally essential, non-essential amino acids [4] (c) Mono/di/trisaccharides + cis vs trans fat [6]

**(a) Secondary structure of protein:**
The secondary structure refers to the local, regular folding pattern of the polypeptide backbone, stabilized primarily by **hydrogen bonds** between the carbonyl (C=O) and amide (N-H) groups of the peptide backbone. Two major types:

1. **Alpha (α) helix:** A right-handed coiled/spiral structure where the polypeptide backbone twists around an imaginary axis; hydrogen bonds form between the C=O of one amino acid and the N-H of the amino acid four residues ahead, stabilizing the helix (e.g., keratin).
2. **Beta (β) pleated sheet:** The polypeptide chain folds back on itself (or aligns with another chain) forming extended, zig-zag strands connected by hydrogen bonds between adjacent strands, either in parallel or antiparallel orientation (e.g., fibroin in silk).

Other secondary structures include **turns** and **loops**, which connect helices and sheets, allowing the polypeptide to change direction.

**(b) Essential, conditionally essential, non-essential amino acids:**

- **Essential amino acids:** Cannot be synthesized by the body and must be obtained through diet. Example: **Lysine**.
- **Conditionally essential amino acids:** Normally synthesized by the body, but under certain conditions (illness, stress, infancy, metabolic disorders) the body cannot produce sufficient amounts, requiring dietary supplementation. Example: **Arginine** (conditionally essential during growth, illness, or trauma).
- **Non-essential amino acids:** Can be synthesized by the body in sufficient quantities from other metabolites, and are not required from the diet. Example: **Alanine**.

**(c) Monosaccharide, Disaccharide, Trisaccharide + cis vs trans fat:**

- **Monosaccharide:** Simplest form of carbohydrate; cannot be hydrolyzed into simpler units. Example: **Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆)**.
- **Disaccharide:** Formed by the condensation of two monosaccharide units with the loss of a water molecule (glycosidic bond). Example: **Sucrose** (glucose + fructose).
- **Trisaccharide:** Formed by the condensation of three monosaccharide units. Example: **Raffinose** (glucose + fructose + galactose).

**Cis fat vs. Trans fat:**

| Feature | Cis fat | Trans fat |
|---|---|---|
| Configuration | Hydrogen atoms on the same side of the double bond | Hydrogen atoms on opposite sides of the double bond |
| Shape | Bent/kinked chain | Straight chain (similar to saturated fats) |
| Source | Naturally occurring in most unsaturated fats (plant oils) | Formed by partial hydrogenation of vegetable oils (also minimally in some animal products) |
| Physical state | Usually liquid at room temperature | Often solid/semi-solid at room temperature |
| Health effect | Generally healthier (part of normal unsaturated fat intake) | Raises LDL ("bad") cholesterol, lowers HDL — linked to heart disease; considered harmful |

### Q11. (a) What is cell cycle? [2] (b) Phases of cell cycle [10] (c) What is Crossing Over? [3]

**(a) Cell cycle:**
The cell cycle is the ordered, repeating sequence of events/stages that a cell undergoes as it grows and divides into two daughter cells, consisting broadly of **Interphase** (growth and DNA replication) and the **Mitotic (M) phase** (nuclear and cell division).

**(b) Phases of the cell cycle:**

**I. Interphase** (longest phase, cell not actively dividing but highly active metabolically):
1. **G1 phase (Gap 1):** Cell grows in size, synthesizes proteins and organelles, preparing for DNA synthesis. Contains the G1 checkpoint, which verifies cell size and DNA integrity before allowing entry into S phase.
2. **S phase (Synthesis):** DNA replication occurs — each chromosome is duplicated to form two sister chromatids joined at the centromere. Chromosome number stays the same, but DNA content doubles.
3. **G2 phase (Gap 2):** Cell continues growing, synthesizes proteins needed for mitosis (e.g., tubulin for spindle fibers). G2 checkpoint verifies DNA replication is complete and error-free before entering mitosis.

**II. M phase (Mitosis + Cytokinesis):**
1. **Prophase:** Chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes (each with two sister chromatids); nuclear envelope begins breaking down; spindle fibers start forming from centrosomes.
2. **Metaphase:** Chromosomes align at the cell's equatorial plate (metaphase plate); spindle fibers attach to centromeres via kinetochores.
3. **Anaphase:** Sister chromatids separate and are pulled toward opposite poles of the cell by shortening spindle fibers.
4. **Telophase:** Chromosomes decondense; nuclear envelope reforms around each set of chromosomes at opposite poles, forming two nuclei.
5. **Cytokinesis:** The cytoplasm divides, producing two genetically identical daughter cells (via cleavage furrow in animal cells, cell plate in plant cells).

**III. G0 phase (Quiescent phase):** Some cells exit the cycle after G1 and enter a resting state (G0), where they perform their specialized functions without dividing further (e.g., most neurons).

**(c) Crossing Over:**
Crossing over is the exchange of genetic material (segments) between non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes, occurring during **Prophase I of meiosis** (specifically the pachytene stage), facilitated by the formation of a synaptonemal complex and structures called **chiasmata**. It results in genetic recombination, producing new allele combinations and increasing genetic variation among offspring.
